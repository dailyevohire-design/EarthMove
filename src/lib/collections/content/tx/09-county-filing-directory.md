# Appendix A — County Filing Directory

This packet ships support for 10 DFW-metro Texas counties. For each, the directory below lists the county appraisal district URL (for looking up owner and legal description) and the county clerk (for filing the lien affidavit). Filing fees, phone numbers, and counter hours vary by county and change; call or check the county website before going in person.

**Rule of thumb:** filing fees in Texas county clerks' offices generally fall in the $25–$40 range for a 1–2 page lien affidavit. Do not rely on a specific number without confirming.

## E-recording

Many Texas county clerks accept e-recording through third-party services (Simplifile, CSC eRecording, ePN). Whether a specific county accepts e-recording, and whether the process is open to walk-in filers, varies; call the clerk before assuming.

## Filing in person

Texas county clerks generally accept walk-in filings during business hours (typically Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 4:30 or 5:00 PM). Bring:
- The notarized lien affidavit (original).
- A check, cash, or card (most counties now accept cards) for the filing fee.
- A photocopy for your records.

The clerk stamps the original with a document number and recording date. Keep the stamped original.

## Filing by mail

Most counties accept filings by mail if you include:
- The notarized lien affidavit (original).
- A check payable to the county clerk.
- A self-addressed stamped envelope for return of the recorded copy.

Use certified mail with return receipt for the mailing itself — you want evidence of timely delivery before the statutory deadline.

## Filing the copy to owner (§ 53.055)

Remember: filing the affidavit with the clerk is not enough. Texas requires you to send a **copy of the filed affidavit to the property owner** (and, for lower-tier claimants, the original contractor) within the statutorily prescribed days. Use certified mail with return receipt. See Step 4 for details.

## Counties outside this list

If your property is in a Texas county not listed above, the process is the same — call the county clerk and confirm the filing fee, accepted methods, and any local formatting requirements. Smaller Texas counties sometimes have quirks (required cover sheets, margin requirements, indexing rules) — call before you drive.

If you have questions about filing in any county, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
