# Red Flags and Gotchas — Texas

Texas lien law has more traps than most states. This section lists the biggest. Read the statute text directly at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov** (Property Code chapter 53) before acting on anything ambiguous.

## § 53.156 fraudulent-lien liability — the Iron Law

Tex. Prop. Code § 53.156 imposes specific damages and attorney fees on a claimant who files a lien affidavit they know to be fraudulent. In practice this includes:
- Liens filed for more than is actually owed (excessive lien).
- Liens filed without adequate lien rights in the first place (wrong tier, no contract, homestead without pre-work contract).
- Liens filed after the statutory deadline.
- Liens filed without the pre-lien notice compliance required for your tier.

Most Texas contractor-payment-liability lawsuits begin with a § 53.156 claim. Treat this as the central risk of filing a lien.

## Slander of title

Separate from § 53.156. A Texas property owner can sue for **slander of title** under common law when an invalid lien is recorded. These claims survive even where § 53.156 may not technically apply, and damages sometimes include the owner's attorney fees and any economic loss.

## Homestead — the single biggest TX trap

Filing a lien on a Texas homestead without complying with the specific § 53.254 homestead-improvement exception (pre-work spouse-signed contract, sometimes filed before work begins) is a textbook § 53.156 case. If you are unsure whether the property is homestead, or whether your contract meets the formalities, do not file without an attorney.

See Step 1a — Is This a Texas Homestead? for the test. If you got the demand-only variant of this kit, the homestead rule is why.

## § 53.056 timing — a specific month's work is forfeited if missed

The § 53.056 pre-lien notice is required **monthly** for subcontractors and suppliers. The deadline is specific: 15th day of the 3rd month after the unpaid month (commercial) or 2nd month after (residential). Missing a month's deadline forfeits lien rights for that month's work.

Good news: missing one month does not necessarily forfeit other months. An attorney can sometimes salvage a partial lien from the months where notice was timely.

## § 53.055 copy-to-owner deadline — often missed

After filing the affidavit, you must send a copy to the property owner within the statutorily prescribed days. Missing this deadline can jeopardize the lien's validity. Most Texas contractors calendar this immediately upon filing.

## Fund-trapping — aggressive; backfires if done wrong

Invoking fund-trapping (§§ 53.081–.084) in a pre-lien notice is powerful but increases the risk of a § 53.156 counterclaim if the underlying claim turns out excessive. Consult an attorney before invoking fund-trapping.

## Subchapter J — 21-day response deadline

If the owner files a Subchapter J petition to summarily remove your lien, you may have as little as 21 days to respond. Miss the deadline, lose the lien. Get an attorney within 48 hours of being served.

## Subchapter H — owner bonding around the lien

If the owner posts a bond (typically 1.5x the lien amount) under Subchapter H, your lien is removed from the property. Your claim is now against the bond, not the land. Attorney territory.

## Retainage rules — separate regime

If your contract has a retainage clause and retainage was not paid, Texas's retainage regime under § 53.101 has separate notice and deadline rules not covered in this kit. Treat retainage as a specialist question.

## Specially fabricated materials (§ 53.058)

If you fabricated materials specifically for this project and they are now sitting in your yard unused because the job was cancelled, § 53.058 has specific notice and filing rules for that scenario.

## Original contractor on homestead — very narrow

Even an original contractor cannot file a homestead improvement lien without the pre-work spouse-signed contract. The homestead exception under § 53.254 applies to all tiers.

## Lien on leased property

If you were hired by a tenant, you generally have lien rights only against the tenant's leasehold interest, not the owner's fee interest — unless the owner was a party to the contract or explicitly authorized the work. This is a fact-specific question; consult an attorney.

## Public-private partnerships

Some modern Texas projects are hybrids — nominally public but operationally private, or vice versa. Lien rights on these depend on the specific structure. Consult an attorney.

## If any of these apply to you

Stop. Call a Texas-licensed attorney before filing. A $300–$700 unbundled document review is cheap compared to § 53.156 or slander-of-title exposure.
