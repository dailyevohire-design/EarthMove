# Step 1b — Your Contractor Tier

Your tier drives the entire Texas lien process. Different tiers have different pre-lien notice requirements, different filing deadlines, and different substantive rights. The four tiers under Tex. Prop. Code ch. 53 are:

1. **Original contractor** — hired directly by the property owner.
2. **Subcontractor** — hired by the original contractor.
3. **Sub-subcontractor** — hired by a subcontractor.
4. **Material supplier** — supplied materials (not labor) to one of the tiers above.

Read the definitions in Tex. Prop. Code §§ 53.001 directly at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov** before picking your tier.

## Tier 1 — Original contractor

You have a direct contract (written or oral) with the property owner. Your rights:

- **Pre-lien notices**: Generally you do **not** send monthly § 53.056 notices on non-homestead, commercial property. The statute carves out original contractors from that requirement. For homestead and residential, the rules are different — read § 53.252 and § 53.254.
- **Filing deadline (commercial non-residential)**: No later than the 15th day of the 4th month after the last day of work.
- **Filing deadline (residential)**: No later than the 15th day of the 3rd month after the last day of work.
- **Copy to owner**: Within 10 days of filing (§ 53.055).

Your template pre-lien notice has a short-form variant that identifies you as the original contractor and serves as a record of the unpaid balance, rather than the full § 53.056 notice. The Step 1 section of this packet discusses when an original contractor uses this variant and when they skip it.

## Tier 2 — Subcontractor

You were hired by the original contractor. Your rights:

- **Pre-lien notices**: You must send monthly notices under § 53.056 to **both** the property owner and the original contractor.
  - **Commercial**: by the 15th day of the **3rd** month after each month of unpaid labor or materials.
  - **Residential**: by the 15th day of the **2nd** month after each month of unpaid labor or materials.
- **Filing deadline**: Same as commercial/residential rules above.
- **Copy to owner and OC**: Within 10 days of filing (§ 53.055).

## Tier 3 — Sub-subcontractor

You were hired by a subcontractor (not the original contractor). Your rights:

- **Pre-lien notices**: Same as Tier 2 subcontractor — monthly notices to both the property owner and the original contractor. You may additionally need to notify the subcontractor who hired you.
- **Filing deadline**: Same as commercial/residential rules above.
- **Copy to owner and OC**: Within 10 days of filing.

## Tier 4 — Material supplier

You supplied materials to the project but did not perform labor. Your rights:

- **Pre-lien notices**: Generally same as subcontractor, but the substantive description in the notice focuses on materials rather than labor. Read § 53.056 and § 53.021 to confirm your specific rights.
- **Filing deadline**: Same as above.
- **Specially fabricated materials** (§ 53.058): If you manufactured materials specifically for this project, a separate notice deadline applies. Read § 53.058 directly.

## Which tier am I?

Most of the time it is obvious. When it is not:

- **I had a contract directly with the owner** → Original contractor.
- **I had a contract with the general contractor who had a contract with the owner** → Subcontractor.
- **I had a contract with a subcontractor who had a contract with a GC** → Sub-subcontractor.
- **I delivered materials to the project but did not install them** → Material supplier.

If your relationship is unusual — you were both a GC and a sub on different parts of the same project, or you were hired by a property manager (not the owner, not a GC), or your contract was oral and the lines are fuzzy — call a Texas-licensed attorney. Tier disputes are common in Texas lien litigation and affect every deadline in this packet.

## Fund trapping (§§ 53.081–.084)

One Texas wrinkle worth knowing about even if you do not use it: the fund-trapping statute lets a subcontractor or supplier effectively require the property owner to withhold payments to the general contractor equal to the claimed amount. To invoke it, you add specific statutory language to your pre-lien notice. Your pre-lien notice template contains a yellow callout referencing this if you want to use it.

Fund-trapping increases the owner's attention but also increases the risk of counterclaims. Most contractors in this situation consult an attorney before invoking fund-trapping.

## Retainage

If your contract has a retainage clause (common in construction), separate notice and deadline rules apply under § 53.101. This packet does not address retainage rules specifically. If retainage applies to your case, call an attorney.

If you are unsure of your tier or any of the above apply, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
