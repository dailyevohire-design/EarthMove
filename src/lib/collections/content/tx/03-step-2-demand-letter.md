# Step 2 — The Demand Letter

Your kit includes a demand letter. Texas statutes referenced here are available at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov**.

 Texas law does not require you to send a demand letter before filing a lien, but most contractors in this situation send one anyway:

1. **It often resolves the dispute.** A clear demand letter delivered by certified mail resolves a meaningful fraction of contractor payment cases without further escalation.
2. **It creates a paper trail.** If you end up in court, the demand letter is evidence of good faith and of a clear opportunity to pay before escalation.
3. **It is cheap.** Roughly $8–$12 for certified mail plus 10 business days.

## When in the process

For subcontractors and suppliers, you will have already sent monthly § 53.056 pre-lien notices (Step 1). The demand letter can go out alongside those, or after a few have been sent and payment has still not been made.

For original contractors, the demand letter is typically the first escalation after informal requests for payment.

## How to send

Certified mail with return receipt requested. See the Certified Mail How-To section.

## What to keep

- A copy of the letter (write the certified mail Article Number on it).
- USPS Form 3800 receipt.
- USPS Form 3811 green card return receipt.
- USPS tracking printout.

## How long to wait

Your demand letter gives the recipient 10 **business days** from receipt to pay. That is the kit's convention; it is not a statutory deadline. Ten business days is about two calendar weeks.

If you do not receive payment within that window, proceed to Step 3 — the lien affidavit — if your fact pattern supports it. (Remember that the hard deadlines from § 53.052 are running regardless of what your demand letter says.)

## Partial payment

If the owner pays part, recalculate the remaining balance. Your lien affidavit (if you file one) must reflect the **current** unpaid balance after all credits and offsets. Overstating is a § 53.156 trap; a lien that names a larger amount than actually owed exposes you to statutory damages.

## Dispute from the owner

If the owner responds with a dispute (the work is defective, the amount is too high, the work was not authorized), stop. A disputed claim is categorically different from a non-response. Proceeding with a lien on a disputed claim significantly increases the risk of a § 53.156 action and a slander-of-title counterclaim. Most contractors in this situation consult a Texas-licensed attorney before filing a lien affidavit when the owner has disputed the amount.

## Optional content: attorney fees and interest

Texas permits certain contractor claims to include attorney fees under § 53.156 and interest at the statutory rate. Your demand letter template does **not** include a specific demand for attorney fees or interest — the yellow callout in the template notes that Texas contractors sometimes include these and prompts you to decide based on your contract and § 53.156 whether to add them before sending.

If you are unsure whether to add attorney fees or interest, leave them out. Adding them and then filing an inflated lien amount is a larger trap than omitting them.

If you have questions about this step, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
