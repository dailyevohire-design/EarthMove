# Step 1 — Pre-Lien Notices

Subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, and material suppliers must send monthly pre-lien notices under Tex. Prop. Code § 53.056 **for each month of unpaid work**. This is the single most common place Texas contractors lose lien rights. Read § 53.056 directly at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov** before serving.

Original contractors on commercial non-residential projects generally do not send § 53.056 notices. See Step 1b for when you skip this step.

## When the notice is due

For **commercial non-residential** projects:
- By the **15th day of the 3rd month** after the month of unpaid labor or materials.
- Example: work performed and unpaid in March → notice due by June 15.
- Count calendar months, not business days.

For **residential** projects:
- By the **15th day of the 2nd month** after the month of unpaid labor or materials.
- Example: work performed and unpaid in March → notice due by May 15.

## Who must be served

- The **property owner** at the owner's last known address.
- The **original contractor** (the party who hired you directly, if you are a subcontractor; the GC even if you were hired by another sub, if you are a sub-sub).

Serve both. Failure to serve either can invalidate the notice.

## How to serve

**Certified mail with return receipt requested** is the standard method. See the Certified Mail How-To section of this packet for step-by-step.

Register the service in your records: keep the green card, keep the USPS Form 3800 receipt, keep the tracking printout. If a later dispute arises over whether you gave notice, your paper trail is your case.

## What the notice contains

Your pre-lien notice template contains the required elements:
- Your name and address.
- The amount unpaid.
- The month and year of unpaid labor or materials.
- A description of the work or materials.
- The property's street address and (where possible) legal description.

Your template also contains a yellow callout directing you to verify the statutory notice language in § 53.056 against the current statute at statutes.capitol.texas.gov. The statute's current form controls; confirm the language matches before serving.

## Monthly cadence

For ongoing non-payment, you must serve a **new notice each month** as the next month's unpaid work passes its deadline. A single notice does not cover multiple months.

In practice: if you performed work in March, April, May, and June that all went unpaid, and you are a subcontractor on a commercial project, you must send four separate § 53.056 notices — one for each month, each by the 15th of its 3rd-month-after deadline.

Set calendar reminders for each month's deadline. Missing one forfeits the lien rights associated with that month's work (but not necessarily the other months).

## Fund trapping (§§ 53.081–.084)

If you want to invoke fund-trapping — which effectively requires the property owner to withhold money from the original contractor equal to your claim — your notice must include specific statutory "trapping" language. The pre-lien notice template in your kit includes a yellow callout pointing to the fund-trapping provisions if you want to use them.

Fund-trapping is aggressive. Most Texas subs and suppliers consult an attorney before invoking it because:
- The GC often responds angrily.
- Invoking fund-trapping in bad faith (if the claim turns out to be excessive) increases § 53.156 exposure.
- The owner sometimes responds by demanding a bond from you under Subchapter H.

## If you missed a deadline

If you missed the notice deadline for a particular month's unpaid work, stop and call a Texas-licensed attorney. You may still have lien rights for subsequent months' work, or you may have a breach-of-contract remedy that is independent of lien rights. An attorney can assess whether any salvage option applies.

## Storage of service records

Keep a folder (physical or digital) titled with the case ID. In it, for each notice sent:
- The notice itself (copy of the mailed original).
- The USPS Form 3800 certified mail receipt (with Article Number).
- The USPS Form 3811 green card return receipt once returned.
- A printout of the USPS tracking page showing delivery.
- Any response or acknowledgment from the recipient.

These are your proof-of-service records. If you ever need to prove you sent the notices, this folder is your case.

If you have questions about any of the above, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
