# Step 4 — After You File

Filing the lien affidavit is not the end. Texas has specific post-filing obligations, and several of the most common § 53.156 liability traps involve the post-filing phase.

## Copy to the owner — § 53.055

Within the statutorily prescribed days after filing (read § 53.055 directly at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov** for the exact number), you must send a copy of the filed affidavit to:

- The **property owner** (at the owner's last known address).
- For subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, and material suppliers: the **original contractor** as well.

Most Texas contractors use certified mail with return receipt for this mailing. Keep the proof.

Missing this deadline can undermine the lien even though the affidavit was timely filed.

## The 1-year foreclosure deadline (§ 53.158)

To actually collect on a Texas lien, you must file a foreclosure lawsuit within one year of the last day of the month in which the statutory period for the lien (commercial vs. residential) ended. The specific deadline depends on the underlying project type; residential projects sometimes carry shorter foreclosure windows. Read § 53.158 for the current rule.

Foreclosure is a district court lawsuit and requires an attorney in nearly all cases. Rough cost range:
- Attorney fees: $4,000–$20,000+ depending on whether contested.
- Court filing fees: ~$300 in Texas district court.
- Process service: $50–$100 per party.

If you are not prepared to sue to foreclose, the recorded lien still has leverage value — it clouds title and often forces the owner's hand at sale or refinance. But if the owner waits out the 1-year deadline, the lien expires.

## When the owner pays

Texas requires a satisfied lien to be released — **§ 53.152**. The release is a separate document (not in this kit) filed with the same county clerk. Blank release forms are available from most county clerks' websites, or an attorney will draft one for $100–$300.

Failing to release a satisfied lien can expose you to:
- A § 53.156 claim for the owner's damages.
- Slander-of-title liability.
- Statutory penalties under § 53.152's release-of-lien timing rules.

Do not skip this step when you get paid.

## Partial payment

If you are paid in part, you typically need to file a **partial release of lien** updating the claim amount. A lien that overstates the remaining balance is a § 53.156 trap.

Partial releases are not in this kit. Most Texas contractors hire an attorney to draft one for $200–$400, or obtain a form from the county clerk's website.

## Disputes, counterclaims, removal petitions

If after filing you are served with:
- A § 53.156 lawsuit.
- A slander-of-title lawsuit.
- A Subchapter J summary removal petition (§ 53.160) — a Texas-specific expedited proceeding to remove an allegedly invalid lien.
- A demand for you to post a bond under Subchapter H.

Stop and engage an attorney immediately. Any of these is a serious legal proceeding with short response deadlines. Do not handle any of them without counsel.

## Subchapter J — expedited removal

Tex. Prop. Code ch. 53 Subchapter J provides an expedited process for a property owner to petition a court to summarily remove an invalid lien. If the owner files a Subchapter J motion, you may have as little as **21 days** to respond. These proceedings are handled by attorneys. If you receive a Subchapter J petition, find an attorney within 48 hours.

## Bond for lien

Tex. Prop. Code ch. 53 Subchapter H allows the owner to post a bond to "bond around" your lien. Once bonded, your claim is no longer attached to the property — it is attached to the bond. The bonding procedure is initiated by the owner, not by you; if it happens, your lien's leverage against the property ends, though your claim survives against the bond.

## What this kit does not cover

- Foreclosure lawsuits (require an attorney).
- Partial-release-of-lien forms (available from county clerks or attorneys).
- Release-of-lien forms for satisfied liens (available from county clerks or attorneys).
- Subchapter J defense (requires an attorney, fast response).
- Subchapter H bond procedures (initiated by owner; claimant defends with an attorney).
- Lis pendens filings during foreclosure (attorney territory).
- Priority disputes with prior-recorded liens (attorney territory).

If any of these apply, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
