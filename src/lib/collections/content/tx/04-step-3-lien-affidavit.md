# Step 3 — Lien Affidavit

The Texas lien affidavit is the document you file with the county clerk to perfect your lien. It lives in **Tex. Prop. Code § 53.054**. Read that section and the surrounding subchapter F directly at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov** before filing.

**This is the document that carries the most legal risk in your kit.** Tex. Prop. Code § 53.156 makes a contractor who files an affidavit they know to be fraudulent liable for specified damages plus attorney fees. Texas also recognizes a common-law slander-of-title claim. A sloppy or inflated affidavit is worse than no affidavit.

## Filing deadlines (hard)

- **Commercial non-residential**: file no later than the **15th day of the 4th month** after the last day of work.
  - Example: last day of work September 10 → filing deadline January 15.
- **Residential**: file no later than the **15th day of the 3rd month** after the last day of work.
  - Example: last day of work September 10 → filing deadline December 15.
- **Retainage** (where applicable under § 53.101): separate, later deadline — read § 53.101 directly if retainage applies.

Missing the deadline forfeits the lien. There are no extensions.

## Required elements of the affidavit

§ 53.054(a) lists the required elements:
1. A sworn statement of the amount claimed.
2. The name and last known address of the owner or reputed owner.
3. A general statement of the kind of work done and materials furnished.
4. The name and last known address of the person by whom the claimant was employed.
5. The name and last known address of the original contractor (if different from the above).
6. A description of the property sufficient to identify it.
7. The claimant's name, mailing address, and (if different) physical address.
8. For sub and supplier affidavits, information about the statutory notices sent.

Your template affidavit contains all these elements; verify each one is filled in correctly before notarizing.

## The property description

Texas requires a description of the property "sufficient to identify it." A street address usually suffices, but a full legal description (lot/block/subdivision or metes-and-bounds) is stronger and always acceptable. See Appendix B — Legal Description Lookup for how to find it.

If your intake did not include a legal description, your affidavit has a yellow callout directing you to Appendix B. Look it up before notarizing.

## Notarization

The lien affidavit must be sworn before a notary public. See the Notary Guide section.

## Filing fee

The filing fee varies by Texas county. Most DFW-metro counties fall in the **$25–$40 range** for a 1–2 page affidavit, but confirm with the specific county clerk before going. See Appendix A — County Filing Directory.

## How to file

Texas county clerks generally accept:
- **In person** during business hours.
- **By mail** with a check for the filing fee and a self-addressed stamped envelope for the recorded copy.
- **Electronically** through Texas's e-filing system for some counties. Not all DFW counties accept e-filed land records; call the clerk before assuming.

File in the **county where the property is located**, not where you or the owner live.

## Copy to the owner — within 10 days of filing (§ 53.055)

This is often missed. Texas requires the lien claimant to send a copy of the filed affidavit to the property owner (and, for lower-tier claimants, to the original contractor) within a specified number of days of filing. Read § 53.055 directly for the exact timing and recipients.

Most Texas contractors use certified mail with return receipt for this copy-to-owner mailing as well. Keep the proof.

## Customer verification callouts

Your lien affidavit template contains yellow "CUSTOMER VERIFICATION REQUIRED" callouts for:
- The statutory affidavit opening language and jurat per § 53.054(a).
- The copy-to-owner deadline and recipients per § 53.055.
- Any missing legal description, owner block, or pre-lien notice compliance summary.

Verify every callout against the current statute before notarizing. If you are unsure on any point, stop and call a Texas-licensed attorney.

## What this kit does not do

- **File for you.** You file.
- **Notarize for you.** You arrange the notary.
- **Draft substitute language.** We do not write around the yellow callouts; you verify the statute yourself, or an attorney does.
- **Remove or bond-off a lien.** Those are procedures for the property owner, not the claimant.

If you have questions about this step, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
