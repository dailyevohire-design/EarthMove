# When to Call a Texas-Licensed Attorney

This kit is designed for straightforward contractor-payment cases on Texas property where you can cleanly identify the fact pattern (tier, homestead status, deadlines). Many Texas cases are not that clean. If your situation has any of the following features, most contractors in similar positions stop and hire an attorney.

## Hard stops — do not use this kit

- **Foreclosure.** To actually collect on the lien by forced sale, you must file a foreclosure lawsuit in Texas district court. Requires an attorney. Rough cost: $4,000–$20,000+.

- **Homestead without a pre-work spouse-signed contract.** See Step 1a. Your kit was delivered as demand-only if this applied. Do not attempt to file a lien under these circumstances — the exposure under § 53.156 is significant.

- **Public property.** Texas mechanic's liens do not attach to public property. Contractors on public works projects rely on payment bonds under Chapter 2253 of the Texas Government Code — different regime, out of scope for this kit.

- **You are past the § 53.056 pre-lien notice deadline for a critical month.** Texas is unforgiving on § 53.056 timing. If you missed it, an attorney is better positioned to assess whether any salvage is possible.

- **You are past the § 53.052 filing deadline.** Hard stop. An attorney may have salvage options (breach of contract, etc.) but the lien itself is gone.

- **You received a Subchapter J petition or a removal motion.** As little as 21 days to respond. Get an attorney within 48 hours.

- **You received a Subchapter H bond notice.** Legal procedure on a short timeline. Get an attorney.

## Slow down and consult an attorney

- **Amount in dispute exceeds $50,000.** The rough threshold at which an attorney consultation (typically $250–$600 in Texas) is cheap insurance.

- **Owner has already sued you** (counterclaim, breach of contract, slander of title).

- **Owner is a government entity.** See "Public property" above.

- **Homestead with a pre-work contract where the signatures or filing are ambiguous.** Homestead lien validity is a § 53.156 trap. An attorney-reviewed affidavit is worth the fee.

- **Multiple contractors claim the same work or different portions.** Priority disputes in Texas often require district court resolution.

- **Fund-trapping**. If you plan to invoke fund-trapping under §§ 53.081–.084, an attorney consultation first is the norm.

- **You're unsure of your tier.** Tier affects every deadline. A 30-minute attorney call clarifies tier for a flat fee.

- **No written contract, and the terms are disputed.** Oral contracts are enforceable in Texas but proving terms is harder.

- **Retainage applies.** Retainage rules under § 53.101 have separate notice and filing procedures not covered in this kit.

- **The project crossed state lines or involved multiple properties.** Separate lien filings required; complex.

- **You want to record a partial release or amendment to the lien.** Drafting requires care to avoid § 53.156 exposure.

## Finding an attorney in Texas

- **State Bar of Texas Lawyer Referral Service**: texasbar.com — 15-minute initial consultation typically $20.
- **Local county bar associations**: Dallas, Tarrant, Collin, Denton all run referral programs.
- **Texas Construction Law sections**: most larger Texas firms have a construction practice. Expect $300–$600/hour for a partner, $150–$300/hour for an associate.
- **Limited-scope representation**: Texas permits "unbundled" legal services. An attorney will review the documents in this kit before you file for $300–$700 without retaining them for the whole case. That is often the most cost-effective step you can take.

## What to expect on an initial consultation

- 30–60 minute meeting.
- The attorney will ask about your tier, contract, homestead status, deadlines, and what you have done so far.
- Expect candor about whether your facts support a lien, whether the lien is worth pursuing, likely cost, and timeline.
- If foreclosure is suggested, ask for fee estimates up front.

This kit is never a substitute for legal advice on a specific fact pattern. If your situation has any of the features above, stop and call a Texas-licensed attorney before filing.
