# Section 0 — Why Your Case Does Not Support A Lien

This is the most important section of your kit. Read it before anything else.

Based on your intake — Texas, homestead property, no pre-work contract signed by both spouses — your situation does not support the filing of a mechanic's lien. Your kit therefore contains two documents, not four: this instruction packet, and a demand letter. The full filing documents (pre-lien notice, lien affidavit) are not included because filing them on your facts would expose you to significant liability under Tex. Prop. Code § 53.156 and to slander-of-title claims.

## Why the homestead rule is different

The Texas Constitution protects the homestead. Under **Tex. Const. art. XVI § 50** and **Tex. Prop. Code § 53.254**, a valid mechanic's lien on a Texas homestead requires, among other things:

- A **written contract** between the property owner and the original contractor, **signed before** work began.
- The contract must be signed by **both spouses**, if the owner is married, regardless of whose name is on the title.
- The contract must have been **filed or recorded** in some cases, depending on the type of improvement.
- Various other formalities depending on the specific type of work.

If any one of these is missing, the mechanic's lien does not attach to the homestead. The Texas Constitution provides that a homestead is protected from forced sale, and the only exceptions are those spelled out in article XVI § 50 — including the narrow homestead-improvement lien, which requires the formalities above.

**Your intake indicated** one or more of the following:
- No original contract signed before work began.
- Contract signed by only one spouse (not both).
- Contract signed by someone other than a record owner.

Any of these forecloses the homestead improvement lien. Filing a lien anyway would likely be invalid, and filing a lien you have reason to know is invalid exposes you to statutory liability under § 53.156 plus slander-of-title damages.

## What you can still do

Your legal remedies are not over. They are different:

- **Demand letter (included in your kit).** Most Texas contractor payment disputes are resolved by a firm demand letter delivered by certified mail with return receipt requested. This is your Step 1 and the single document in your kit you can send today.

- **Breach of contract lawsuit.** You can sue the owner in civil court for breach of contract. Requires an attorney unless the amount is small enough for small claims.
  - Cost: $3,000–$15,000+ in attorney fees for a contested case.
  - Timeline: 6–18 months to judgment.

- **Small claims (Justice of the Peace) court.** If the amount is **$20,000 or less**, you can sue in Texas Justice of the Peace court without an attorney. Filing fees are typically $50–$75; you represent yourself.
  - Good option for claims up to $20,000.
  - https://www.txcourts.gov/ has links to Justice Court procedures.

- **Texas Attorney General Consumer Protection Division.** If you believe the owner's conduct crosses into consumer fraud or deceptive practices, you can file a complaint at **https://oag.my.site.com**. This does not get you paid directly, but it sometimes prompts resolution.

- **Mediation.** Private mediation runs $500–$2,000 and often resolves a dispute that would take $15,000 to litigate.

## Why this kit is still worth what you paid

The demand letter, sent correctly by certified mail, resolves a meaningful share of contractor payment disputes without further escalation. That is the most likely outcome in your case — the owner pays when they see the letter and understand you are serious.

If the demand letter does not produce payment, you have the information you need to decide your next step: small claims, full litigation, or mediation. Each is explained in the next sections of this packet.

## Do not file a lien on a Texas homestead without counsel

Do not attempt to file a lien affidavit on a Texas homestead property without a Texas-licensed attorney reviewing your facts. Homestead-exception filings are narrow and technical. Filing one that turns out to be invalid exposes you to a § 53.156 action with mandatory penalties.

If you have any doubt whether the homestead bar applies to your facts, call a Texas-licensed attorney before using any document from this kit beyond the demand letter.
