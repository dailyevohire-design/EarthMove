# Notary Guide

The Texas lien affidavit must be sworn before a notary public. Without a valid jurat, the affidavit is invalid on its face and will be rejected by the county clerk — or, worse, accepted for recording and then attacked later under § 53.156.

## What a notary does

A Texas notary public verifies your identity, witnesses your signature, and applies an official seal. For a lien affidavit, the notary administers a jurat: you swear under oath that the contents of the affidavit are true.

The notary does **not** verify that the contents are actually true. They verify only that you signed and swore. Lying to a notary under oath is perjury under Texas Penal Code ch. 37.

## What to bring

- The lien affidavit, **unsigned**. Do not sign it before you see the notary. The notary will watch you sign.
- Government-issued photo ID: Texas driver's license, state ID, passport, or other qualifying ID under Texas Government Code § 406.013.
- Payment. See costs below.

## Where to find a notary in Texas

Ranked by typical cost:

- **Your bank** — Often free for customers at major Texas banks and credit unions. Call ahead.
- **AAA office** — Free for AAA members (many Texas locations).
- **UPS Store / FedEx Office** — Typically $5–$12 per notarization under Texas statutory maximums. Walk-in; consistent availability.
- **Credit union** — Usually free for members.
- **Law firm reception** — Often $5–$15; call ahead.
- **Mobile notary** — $25–$75, the notary comes to you. Useful near the filing deadline.

Texas law caps the maximum notary fee per notarization at $6 per signature (Government Code § 406.024), but in practice retail locations add a service fee for the administrative time, often bundled into the $5–$25 range.

## The appointment

About 5 minutes end-to-end:

1. You hand the notary the unsigned affidavit.
2. The notary checks your ID.
3. The notary administers the oath: "Do you swear or affirm that the contents of this document are true?"
4. You answer "I do" or "yes."
5. You sign in front of the notary.
6. The notary completes the jurat block — date, county, the notary's signature, printed name, and commission expiration date.
7. The notary affixes the Texas notary seal (stamp or embossed).

That's it.

## The jurat

Your Texas lien affidavit ends with a jurat block — the form that says "Subscribed and sworn to before me…" The notary will recognize it. If the notary tells you the form is wrong or incomplete, do not argue; the notary's objection is worth listening to. Call a Texas-licensed attorney to adjust the form.

## After notarization

The affidavit is now ready to file. Do not lose the original — the county clerk needs the original with the wet notary seal. Make photocopies for your records before filing.

## E-notarization

Texas permits remote online notarization under Government Code § 406.101. If you use a remote online notary, confirm:
- The notary is commissioned as a Texas online notary public.
- The identity verification procedure meets state standards (typically knowledge-based authentication + ID document verification).
- The e-signed, e-notarized document will be accepted by the specific county clerk where you plan to file. Call the clerk to confirm before using this path.

For most contractors using this kit, the cheapest and most reliable path is walk-in notarization at a UPS Store, bank, or AAA office.

If you have questions about this step, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
