# Step 1a — Is This a Texas Homestead?

**This is the most important section of your Texas kit.** The homestead rule drives everything. If the property is a homestead, your options narrow significantly, and in many cases the only document that goes out is a demand letter.

Homestead protection in Texas comes from **Tex. Const. art. XVI §§ 50 and 51** and is implemented in **Tex. Prop. Code ch. 41** (homestead designation) and **Tex. Prop. Code § 53.254** (mechanic's liens on homestead). Read those sections directly at **https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov** before making a call.

## The plain-English test

A property is a Texas homestead if **all** of the following are true:

1. The property is **occupied by the owner as their primary residence** (not a rental, not a vacation home, not an investment property).
2. The property meets the **acreage limits**:
   - **Urban homestead**: up to 10 acres, must be in a city, town, or village.
   - **Rural homestead**: up to 100 acres for a single adult, up to 200 acres for a family.
3. The owner has **not abandoned** the homestead.

A homestead does not need to be "designated" in a filed document to count as a homestead. Occupation and intent are enough. A property can be a homestead even if the county assessor does not list it as such.

## If the property is a homestead

You then need to answer a second question: **does the homestead improvement exception apply?** This is covered in § 53.254 and requires:

1. A **written contract** between the property owner and the original contractor, **signed before work began**.
2. The contract must be signed by **both spouses**, if the owner is married (regardless of whose name is on the title).
3. For certain work types, the contract must be **filed with the county clerk** before work began.

If all three are met, a homestead improvement lien is possible under the narrow § 53.254 exception. This kit supports that case — your full-kit variant contains documents tailored to it.

If any one of the three is missing, the lien is **not available**. Your kit was delivered as a demand-only variant. Read **Section 0 — Why Your Case Does Not Support A Lien** (the first section in your packet) for what your options still are.

## If the property is **not** a homestead

Good — your kit proceeds as a full kit with the standard pre-lien notice + lien affidavit process. Continue to Step 1b — Your Contractor Tier.

Common situations that are **not** a homestead:
- Commercial or industrial property.
- Rental properties the owner does not live in.
- Vacation homes the owner does not primarily occupy.
- Land under development, not yet occupied.
- Property owned by an entity (LLC, corporation) — entities cannot claim homestead.

## Edge cases that require an attorney

- **The owner is separated or divorced** and one spouse is claiming homestead and the other is not.
- **The property is held in a trust** — the homestead question turns on the trust's structure.
- **The property is partially homestead** — a dual-use property with both a residence and a commercial rental, or a residence on 50 acres of unrelated land.
- **The owner is a recent purchaser** who has not yet moved in.
- **You were hired by a tenant, not the owner** on a property the owner claims as homestead.

Any of these, call a Texas-licensed attorney. Homestead questions generate more attorney-fee liability than any other single issue in Texas lien law.

## How to verify your intake answer

During intake you told us whether the property is a homestead and whether a pre-work spouse-signed contract exists. Double-check both:

- **Homestead status**: Is the owner actually living there as their primary residence? Can you prove it if challenged? Utility bills, voter registration, driver's license all typically point to the same address for a homestead.

- **Pre-work contract**: Do you have the original written contract, signed by the owner (and the spouse, if married), **dated before** your first day of work? If the contract was signed the day work began, that typically counts, but any contract signed after work began does not satisfy the homestead-improvement exception.

If either answer is uncertain, treat the case as homestead-no-contract (demand-only). Sending a demand letter carries no lien-law liability; filing an invalid homestead lien carries significant liability under § 53.156.

If you have any doubt, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
