# Appendix B — Legal Description Lookup

A Texas lien affidavit must describe the property "sufficient to identify it" (Tex. Prop. Code § 53.054(a)(6)). A street address usually suffices, but a formal legal description (lot/block/subdivision or metes-and-bounds) is stronger and always acceptable.

## What a legal description is

A legal description is the formal, unique identifier for a parcel. In Texas:

1. **Lot / Block / Subdivision** — most common in platted residential subdivisions. Example: "Lot 12, Block A, Oak Park Addition, an addition to the City of Dallas, Dallas County, Texas."

2. **Metes and bounds** — common for unplatted tracts and rural land. Long technical description with compass directions, distances, and references to surveys and section lines.

The street address alone (e.g. "900 Market Street") is not a legal description. If you can get the lot/block/subdivision or metes-and-bounds, use it.

## Where to find the legal description

- **County appraisal district website** — Every Texas county has an "appraisal district" (CAD) that publishes parcel records online, searchable by street address. Legal description is on the parcel detail page. See Appendix A for your county's CAD URL.
- **Your contract paperwork** — If you had a written contract with the owner, it may include the legal description or reference a recorded deed.
- **The owner's deed** — Recorded in the county clerk's real property records. Public, searchable by owner name.
- **Title insurance policy** — Schedule A contains the legal description. Useful if you worked on new construction and received title paperwork.
- **The property tax statement** — Owners receive one annually; it includes the legal description.

## Step-by-step: Dallas County example

1. Go to **https://www.dallascad.org/**.
2. Click "Property Search" and enter the street address.
3. Open the parcel detail page.
4. Find the "Legal Description" field.
5. Copy it verbatim — including abbreviations, numbers, and punctuation.
6. Paste into the legal description field of your lien affidavit, replacing any yellow CUSTOMER VERIFICATION callout.

Other DFW counties (Tarrant, Denton, Collin, Rockwall, Kaufman, Ellis, Johnson, Parker, Wise) follow the same pattern. See Appendix A — County Filing Directory for each CAD URL.

## What to do if you cannot find it

- **Call the CAD** — Most Texas county appraisal districts will read the legal description over the phone given a street address. Call is usually handled in under 5 minutes. No charge.
- **Request a copy of the deed** from the county clerk — Fees vary, typically $1–$2 per page.
- **Order a title search** from a local title company — $100–$300. Overkill for most kits.

## Filing without a legal description

Texas courts have accepted lien affidavits that describe the property by street address alone when the description is otherwise sufficient to identify the land. But "sufficient to identify" is a fact-specific test, and a lien described only by street address is more vulnerable to challenge — particularly in counties with multiple properties at the same address (common with apartment complexes, subdivided tracts, etc.).

Most Texas contractors in this situation take the extra hour to find the legal description rather than rely on the street-address fallback. If you decide to file without it, expect the lien to be more vulnerable, and consider an attorney consultation first.

If you have questions about this step, call a Texas-licensed attorney.
