# Notary Guide

The Statement of Mechanic's Lien must be sworn before a notary public. Without a valid notary acknowledgment, the lien is invalid on its face and will be rejected (or, worse, accepted for recording and then attacked later). This section explains how to get it done cheaply and correctly.

## What a notary does

A notary public verifies your identity, witnesses your signature, and applies an official seal. For a mechanic's lien, the notary acknowledges that you personally appeared, that you were identified, and that you swore (under oath or affirmation) that the contents of the lien statement are true.

The notary **does not** verify that the contents are actually true. They verify only that you signed it, and they rely on your sworn statement for the truth. Lying to a notary under oath is perjury.

## What to bring

- The lien statement, **unsigned**. Do not sign it before you see the notary. The notary will watch you sign.
- Government-issued photo ID: Colorado driver's license, state ID, passport, or military ID.
- Payment. See costs below.

## Where to find a notary

Ranked by typical cost:

- **Your bank** — Free for customers at most banks and credit unions. Call ahead to confirm availability; not every branch staffs a notary at all hours.
- **AAA office** — Free for AAA members (many offices; call ahead for availability).
- **UPS Store / FedEx Office** — $5–$25 per signature, walk-in, consistent availability. A common choice for contractors who do not bank with a notary-providing bank.
- **Credit union** — Often free for members.
- **Law firm receptionist** — Often $5–$15, generally walk-in during business hours.
- **Mobile notary** — $25–$75, the notary comes to you. Useful if you are elderly, disabled, or running out the 4-month clock.

## What happens during notarization

The whole appointment takes about 5 minutes.

1. You hand the notary the unsigned document.
2. The notary looks at your ID and asks your name.
3. The notary asks you to raise your right hand and swear or affirm that the contents of the document are true.
4. You sign in front of the notary.
5. The notary fills in the acknowledgment block, signs, and applies their seal.

That is the whole procedure. The notary will write the date, the county, their signature, their printed name, and the expiration of their commission.

## The jurat vs. acknowledgment

Your lien statement uses a **jurat** — the form where you swear under oath to the truth of the contents. The notary will recognize the block at the bottom of the document. If the notary tells you the form is wrong or incomplete, do not argue; the notary's objection is worth listening to. Call a Colorado-licensed attorney to adjust the form.

## After notarization

The lien statement is now ready to file. Do not lose the original — the recorder will need the original with the wet notary seal. Make photocopies for your records before filing.

If you have questions about this step, call a Colorado-licensed attorney.
