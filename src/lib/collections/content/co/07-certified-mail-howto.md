# Certified Mail How-To

Your demand letter and Notice of Intent both need to be served by certified mail with return receipt requested. This section walks through the exact USPS procedure. The whole process takes about 10 minutes at a post office counter.

## What to bring to the post office

- The sealed envelope, addressed to the recipient.
- Two USPS forms, which you fill out at the counter: **Form 3800** (certified mail receipt — green and white) and **Form 3811** (return receipt — the "green card").
- Payment. Estimated total cost: roughly $8–$12 per mailing for a standard letter-sized envelope.

## Filling out Form 3800 (certified mail receipt)

- Write the recipient's name and address in the block at the top.
- Leave the Article Number / tracking barcode alone — it is pre-printed.
- Remove the sticker from the top of the form and stick it to the envelope, above the recipient's address.
- Keep the bottom portion of the form (the "receipt") — it has the Article Number printed on it.

## Filling out Form 3811 (return receipt, the "green card")

- On the front of the green card, write the recipient's name and address.
- Check the "Certified Mail" box.
- On the back of the green card, write **your** name and address — this is where the signed card will be returned after delivery.
- Write the Article Number from Form 3800 on the green card (so the two forms are matched).
- Seal the green card into the pouch on the back of the envelope, OR attach it to the back with the adhesive strip. USPS clerks will show you if unclear.

## What the costs cover

- Certified mail fee: approximately $4–$5.
- Return receipt (green card mailed back to you): approximately $3–$4.
- First-class postage for a 1 oz letter: currently around $0.73.

The totals above are approximate; call your post office or check usps.com for current rates.

## Tracking delivery

Every certified mail piece has a unique 20-digit Article Number on Form 3800. Enter it at https://tools.usps.com/go/TrackConfirmAction to see delivery status in real time. The tracking page shows:
- Acceptance at the sending post office.
- Arrival at the destination post office.
- Delivery date and time (and, if signed, the recipient's signature status).

Print the tracking page once delivery is confirmed. It is acceptable secondary evidence of delivery if the green card is lost in the mail.

## When the green card comes back

A few days to two weeks after delivery, USPS mails the green card back to you with:
- The recipient's signature (or "Agent" if signed by someone other than the addressee).
- The date of delivery.

**The date of delivery is what starts the 10-day NOI clock** for Step 2. File the green card with your project records.

## If the green card does not come back

Occasionally USPS loses the green card on its return trip. The tracking page printout (showing "Delivered") is sufficient proof in most Colorado filing contexts, but not in all. Most contractors in this situation are fine with the tracking printout; if your case is contested and service is questioned, call an attorney.

## If the mail is refused or unclaimed

"Refused" mail is typically treated as delivered for service purposes. "Unclaimed" mail (held at the post office for the statutory period and then returned) is more complicated. In either case, keep the returned envelope, unopened, with the USPS notations intact. Most contractors in this situation consult an attorney before proceeding with the lien when service was refused or unclaimed.

## Alternative methods (generally stronger, more expensive)

- **Registered Mail**: The most secure USPS service. Expensive ($15–$25+) and slow. Rarely used for mechanic's lien contexts; certified mail with return receipt is the practical default.
- **Personal service by a process server**: $50–$100 per recipient. Creates the strongest record of service (affidavit of service).
- **Sheriff's service**: Varies by county. Slower but statutorily recognized.

## What not to use

- **Certificate of Mailing** — cheaper than certified mail but only proves you mailed it, not that anyone received it. Insufficient for the NOI.
- **Regular first-class mail** — no proof of delivery. Can supplement certified mail but cannot replace it.

If you have questions about this step, call a Colorado-licensed attorney.
