# Step 1 — The Demand Letter

The demand letter is the first document in your kit. Colorado statutes cited here are available at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes**.

 Colorado law does not require you to send a demand letter before filing a lien, but most contractors in this situation send one anyway, for three reasons:

1. **It often resolves the dispute.** A well-drafted demand letter, delivered by certified mail with return receipt requested, frequently prompts payment without further escalation. Contractors using kits like this one report that a sizable fraction of disputes are resolved at this stage.
2. **It creates a paper trail.** If you end up in court, the demand letter is evidence that you gave the other side a clear opportunity to pay.
3. **It is cheap.** Roughly $8–$12 in certified mail costs, plus the 10 business days you wait for a response.

## How to send the demand letter

**Certified mail with return receipt requested** is the method most Colorado contractors use. Your local post office will fill out the green USPS Form 3811 ("green card") and the white-and-green USPS Form 3800 ("certified mail slip"). Keep both. The Article Number from Form 3800 lets you track delivery online at usps.com. The signed green card comes back to you in the mail after delivery.

You can also send a duplicate by regular first-class mail or email as a parallel channel. Do not substitute those for certified mail; the certified-mail return receipt is the piece of evidence that matters.

## What to keep

For every demand letter you send, keep:
- A copy of the letter itself (write the certified mail Article Number on the copy).
- The USPS Form 3800 certified mail receipt.
- The USPS Form 3811 return receipt (the green card) once it comes back.
- A printout of the USPS tracking page showing the date of delivery.

## How long to wait

Your demand letter gives the other side ten (10) business days from receipt to pay. That is a window this kit uses to keep the process moving; it is not a statutory deadline. Ten business days is about two calendar weeks. If the tenth business day lands on a weekend or holiday, wait until the next business day.

If the ten business days pass and you have received no response, move on to Step 2 — the Notice of Intent to Lien.

## What if they pay partially?

If they pay some but not all, most contractors in this situation either (a) send a second demand letter for the remaining balance before escalating, or (b) proceed with the Notice of Intent for the remaining balance. The amount on your NOI and lien statement should reflect the **current** unpaid balance after all credits and offsets (C.R.S. § 38-22-109(1) specifically requires the lien amount to account for credits and offsets). Overstating the amount can trigger liability under § 38-22-128.

## What if they dispute the claim?

If you receive a response disputing the amount or the work, stop and read the "When to Call an Attorney" section of this packet. A disputed claim is a different animal than a non-response. Proceeding with a lien when there is a good-faith dispute about the amount can expose you to liability. Most contractors in this situation consult an attorney before filing a lien on a disputed claim.

If you have questions about this step, call a Colorado-licensed attorney.
