# Colorado Mechanic's Lien Process Overview

Colorado's mechanic's lien statute lives in the Colorado Revised Statutes, Title 38, Article 22. The statute text is free and public. Most contractors in this situation benefit from reading the two or three sections that apply to their fact pattern directly at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes**. Search for "Title 38" and open Article 22.

## The process in chronological order

The numbers below are the law's deadlines. Missing any one of them can forfeit your lien rights.

**Day 0 — Last day of work.**
The statute of limitations clock starts the day you stopped providing labor or materials. Two deadlines run from this day:
- 4 months to file the Statement of Mechanic's Lien with the county recorder (C.R.S. § 38-22-109(5)).
- 6 months to file a lawsuit to foreclose the lien, or the lien expires (C.R.S. § 38-22-110).

**Days 1–10 — Send the demand letter.**
The demand letter is not required by statute but is strong evidence of good faith and solves the dispute without escalation in a large share of cases (industry estimates vary; most contractors who use this kit report a meaningful fraction of cases pay at this stage). Send it by certified mail with return receipt requested. Wait 10 business days.

**Days 11–20 — Serve the Notice of Intent to File a Mechanic's Lien.**
Per C.R.S. § 38-22-109(3), you must serve a 10-day notice of intent on the property owner and (if you are a subcontractor or supplier) the principal contractor before filing the lien statement. Certified mail with return receipt requested is the standard method. Wait 10 days from the date of receipt (not the date mailed).

**Days 21+ — File the Statement of Mechanic's Lien.**
This must be filed in person or (in some counties) electronically at the county recorder's office in the county where the property is located. It must be notarized (sworn before a notary public). The lien must be filed within 4 months of the last day of work.

**Within 6 months of the last day of work — decide on foreclosure.**
To collect on the lien, you must file a foreclosure lawsuit in Colorado district court within 6 months of the last day of work. If you do not file, the lien expires by operation of law. Foreclosure requires an attorney. Rough cost range: $3,000 to $15,000+ in attorney fees depending on whether the owner contests.

## Side notes

- **Priority**: A mechanic's lien in Colorado generally relates back to the date the work first commenced on the project. Earlier-recorded mortgages typically take priority; later-recorded ones typically do not. If the project was new construction on raw land, this works in your favor; on a remodel, it usually does not.
- **Subcontractors and suppliers**: You have the same deadlines as the original contractor. The NOI must be served on both the property owner and the principal contractor.
- **Public property**: Colorado mechanic's liens do not attach to property owned by governmental entities. Contractors on public projects rely on payment bonds under C.R.S. § 38-26-105 instead — out of scope for this kit.

If you are unsure whether your facts fit the process above, call a Colorado-licensed attorney before using the filing documents in this kit.
