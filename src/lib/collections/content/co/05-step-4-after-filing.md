# Step 4 — After You File

Filing the lien is not the end of the process. The statute text for every Colorado section referenced in this step is available at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes** — open Title 38 Article 22.

 The lien is a passive encumbrance — it clouds title, which often prompts payment, but it does not force payment by itself. To actually collect on a Colorado mechanic's lien, you must either be paid voluntarily by the owner (typical outcome in most cases) or file a foreclosure lawsuit.

## Notify the owner

Most Colorado contractors, after filing, send a copy of the recorded lien to the property owner by certified mail with return receipt requested. This is not a strict statutory requirement, but it is the step that typically produces payment: the owner cannot sell, refinance, or clear title without addressing the lien, and a copy in their hands shows it is actually on record.

Include a cover letter noting the reception number and recording date. Keep proof of service.

## The 6-month foreclosure deadline

C.R.S. § 38-22-110 requires a foreclosure lawsuit to be filed within 6 months of the last day of work, or the lien expires by operation of law.

Foreclosure is a lawsuit in Colorado district court (or county court if the amount in controversy is within its jurisdiction). It is not something most contractors handle without an attorney. Rough cost range:
- Attorney fees: $3,000–$15,000+ depending on whether the owner contests.
- Court filing fees: ~$250 for most district court cases.
- Process service: $50–$100 per party served.

If you are not prepared to file a foreclosure lawsuit, consider whether filing the lien is worth it for you. A recorded lien still has leverage value — it clouds title — but if the owner calls your bluff by waiting out the 6-month clock, the lien evaporates.

## When the owner pays

When you are paid in full, Colorado does not have a single statutory deadline for releasing a lien, but you owe the owner a written release of lien filed with the same county recorder. Releases of lien are not included in this kit; most county recorder websites publish a blank Release of Lien form, or you can hire an attorney to draft one for $100–$300.

Failing to release a satisfied lien can expose you to liability under C.R.S. § 38-22-128 and to slander-of-title claims. Do not skip this step when you get paid.

## When the owner pays partially or disputes

If the owner pays some but not all, reduce the lien amount and file an amended lien (or, in some counties, file a partial release). Overstating the remaining balance on a lien can trigger § 38-22-128 liability. Most contractors in this situation engage an attorney to draft the amendment or partial release.

If the owner responds with a counterclaim, a dispute letter, or threats of a § 38-22-128 action or slander-of-title suit, stop and call an attorney. You may have a strong position, but assessing it requires a lawyer's evaluation of your fact pattern.

## What this kit does not cover

- Foreclosure lawsuits (require an attorney).
- Partial-release-of-lien forms (available from county recorders or attorneys).
- Bonding around a lien under C.R.S. § 38-22-131 (owner procedure, not claimant's procedure).
- Lis pendens filings during foreclosure (attorney territory).
- Disputes over priority vs. earlier-recorded mortgages (attorney territory).
- Public property liens and payment bond claims under C.R.S. § 38-26-105.

If any of these apply to your situation, call a Colorado-licensed attorney.
