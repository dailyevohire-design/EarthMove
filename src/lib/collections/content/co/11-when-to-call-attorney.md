# When to Call a Colorado-Licensed Attorney

This kit is designed for straightforward contractor-payment disputes on private, commercial, industrial, and non-homestead residential property in Colorado. The statutes referenced below live in C.R.S. Title 38 Article 22 — read the public text at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes** when the discussion below raises a section that applies to your facts. If your situation has any of the following features, most contractors in similar positions stop and hire an attorney before filing.

## Hard stops — do not use this kit

- **Foreclosure.** If you need to actually collect on the lien by forcing a sale, you must file a foreclosure lawsuit in district court. This requires an attorney. Rough cost: $3,000 to $15,000+, depending on complexity and whether the owner contests. Foreclosure is the one scenario where the legal fees typically exceed what this kit cost by two to three orders of magnitude.

- **Public property.** Mechanic's liens do not attach to property owned by governmental entities in Colorado. Contractors on public projects rely on payment bonds under C.R.S. § 38-26-105 — a different legal regime out of scope for this kit.

- **You received a Notice of Non-Responsibility before starting work.** C.R.S. § 38-22-105 describes a procedure by which a property owner (usually a landlord) can disclaim responsibility for work contracted for by a tenant. If you saw one of these before starting, your lien rights against the owner are probably limited.

- **You are past any deadline.** If the 4-month filing deadline has already passed, or the 6-month foreclosure deadline is too close, an attorney is better positioned to advise on salvage options (breach-of-contract suit, small claims, etc.).

## Slow down and consult an attorney

- **Amount in dispute exceeds $50,000.** The rough dividing line between "use the kit" and "hire an attorney" for most contractors. Above $50,000 the cost/benefit of a $300–$600 attorney consultation is lopsided in favor of the consultation.

- **Owner has already sued you.** Defending a lawsuit while also filing a lien is a two-front fight. Hire an attorney.

- **Owner is a government entity.** See "Public property" above.

- **Property is a homestead with a pre-existing lien.** Priority disputes get complicated fast. Call a lawyer.

- **Multiple parties claim the same work.** If another contractor, a supplier, or a union trust fund is also claiming an interest in the same work, you need counsel to sort out priority.

- **You're unsure whether you have the right to file a lien.** Lien rights depend on whether you were hired by a party in a lien-protected class. If you worked on spec without a contract, or as a favor, or as a labor pool to another sub, your rights may be weaker than you think.

- **Owner has threatened a § 38-22-128 countersuit or slander-of-title claim.** If they are serious, you need counsel. These are real causes of action and can produce larger judgments than your underlying claim.

- **You want to record a partial release.** Partial releases (common when you have been paid some but not all) need to be drafted precisely. Attorneys do this for $100–$300.

- **The project involved multiple properties or multiple owners.** Separate lien filings required; more complex.

- **You do not have a written contract.** Oral contracts are enforceable in Colorado, but proving the terms and establishing your lien rights is harder. An attorney consultation is worth the price.

## Finding an attorney

- **Colorado Bar Association Lawyer Referral Service**: cobar.org → Find a Lawyer. Initial consultation rate is typically $50 for 30 minutes.
- **Local county bar association**: Denver, El Paso, Boulder, and Larimer counties run lawyer referral programs with reduced-fee consultations.
- **Colorado Construction Law sections**: most larger Colorado firms have a construction practice group. Expect $250–$500/hour for a partner, $150–$300/hour for an associate.
- **Limited-scope representation**: Colorado permits attorneys to take on individual tasks ("unbundled services"). You can hire an attorney to review the documents in this kit before you file for $200–$500 without retaining them for the whole case.

## What to expect on an initial consultation

- 30–60 minute meeting.
- The attorney will ask about your contract, the work performed, the amount in dispute, the deadlines, and what has happened so far.
- Expect candor about whether your facts support a lien, whether the lien is worth pursuing, and what the likely cost and timeline are.
- If the attorney suggests foreclosure, ask for the estimated fee range up front.

This kit is never a substitute for legal advice on a specific fact pattern. If your situation has any of the features above, stop and call a Colorado-licensed attorney before filing.
