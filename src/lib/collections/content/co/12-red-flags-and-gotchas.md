# Red Flags and Gotchas — Colorado

Colorado's mechanic's lien statute has traps that catch contractors using kits like this one every year. This section lists the most common and tells you where to look in the statute. Read at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes**, search for "Title 38 Article 22" for the relevant text.

## § 38-22-128 excessive-lien liability — double damages

The single biggest trap. Under C.R.S. § 38-22-128, a contractor who files a lien for an excessive amount (more than is actually owed) or knowingly invalid can be liable to the owner for **twice the actual damages** plus costs and reasonable attorney fees. "Excessive" includes overstating by a few percent.

The safest practice: on the day you sign the lien statement, recalculate the unpaid balance from scratch. Deduct every partial payment. Deduct offsets for disputed line items. If you are not sure whether to include a line item, leave it out — it is better to file low than high.

## Slander of title

Separate from § 38-22-128. A Colorado property owner who believes your lien is invalid can sue you for **slander of title** — a tort claim for the damages caused by the cloud on title. These claims typically include the owner's attorney fees and any economic loss (e.g. a sale that fell through because of the lien). Slander-of-title claims often settle in the $5,000–$50,000 range but can go much higher.

## Priority vs. earlier-recorded mortgages

A mechanic's lien in Colorado generally relates back to the date work first began on the project. If the property had a recorded mortgage before your work began, the mortgage almost certainly takes priority over your lien. In practice this means: if the property is foreclosed by the mortgage holder, your lien gets whatever is left after the mortgage is paid — usually nothing.

This does not mean the lien is useless. The encumbrance still clouds title and often prompts payment. But do not expect to collect through foreclosure against a mortgaged property unless the property has substantial equity.

## Bonding around the lien — § 38-22-131

A property owner can pay cash or post a surety bond equal to 1.5x the lien amount into court and get the lien released from the title. Your claim is not extinguished — it is now against the bond, not the property — but the leverage effect of the lien on the owner's ability to sell or refinance is gone.

Most owners do not use this procedure (it is expensive and slow) but sophisticated commercial owners sometimes do. If it happens, hire an attorney to pursue the bond claim.

## Disburser's notice — § 38-22-126

A "disburser" (typically a title company or construction lender handling draw payments) can record a notice that requires subcontractors and suppliers to provide notice of their claims directly to the disburser in order to preserve lien rights. If a disburser's notice has been filed on your project, you may have a narrower window to notify the disburser than the general NOI process in this kit contemplates. Most subs and suppliers in this situation consult counsel before filing.

## Release on payment — do not skip

If you are paid in full or in part, you owe the owner a release of the lien. C.R.S. § 38-22-128 includes a provision for damages against a contractor who fails to timely release a satisfied lien. Do not skip the release when you get paid.

A blank Release of Lien form is available from most county recorders' websites, or an attorney will draft one for $100–$300.

## "Extras" and change orders

Lien amounts include extras and change orders **only if** they were part of the original contract or otherwise properly authorized. If you added $15,000 of extras mid-project without written approval, and you include them in your lien amount, you invite a § 38-22-128 challenge.

Practice: the lien amount should match the invoices the owner has received. Do not inflate with charges that have not been invoiced.

## Commencement date ambiguity

"First day work commenced on the project" determines lien priority against mortgages and can matter in multi-contractor projects. If your first day does not match the project's first day (because, e.g., you did post-frame cleanup after another sub finished), do not claim a commencement date earlier than your own first day — that is its own form of excessive lien exposure.

## If any of these apply to you

Stop. Call a Colorado-licensed attorney before filing. The attorney consultation (typically $200–$500 for an unbundled review of the documents) is cheap compared to § 38-22-128 exposure.
