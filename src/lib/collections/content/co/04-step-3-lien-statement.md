# Step 3 — Statement of Mechanic's Lien

The Statement of Mechanic's Lien is the third filing document in your kit. It is filed with the county recorder's office in the county where the property is located. Once recorded, it clouds title and shows up on any title search — which is usually what prompts payment.

**This is the document that carries the most legal risk.** C.R.S. § 38-22-128 makes the contractor liable for damages (including attorney fees) if the lien is excessive, false, or filed knowing the claim is invalid. A sloppy lien is worse than no lien.

## Deadlines (hard, no extensions)

- **4 months from the last day of work** to file the lien statement with the county recorder, per C.R.S. § 38-22-109(5).
- **6 months from the last day of work** to file a foreclosure lawsuit, per C.R.S. § 38-22-110. If you do not file, the lien expires.

Most contractors in this situation calendar both dates the day they start the process.

## Required elements of the lien statement

C.R.S. § 38-22-109(1) sets out the required elements. Read the statute text directly at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes** before filing. In summary, the statement must:

1. Identify the claimant (you / your company) with name and address.
2. Identify the owner of record with name and address.
3. Identify the party against whom the lien is claimed (the principal contractor, if you are a sub).
4. Describe the property by legal description (lot/block/subdivision or metes-and-bounds). A street address alone is generally insufficient — see Appendix B.
5. Describe the labor and materials furnished.
6. State the first and last days you furnished labor or materials.
7. State the amount due after all just credits and offsets.
8. Be sworn to (verified) before a notary public.

## The legal description

Your lien statement requires the legal description of the property — not just the street address. If you did not enter the legal description during intake, your lien document has a yellow callout directing you to Appendix B. Look it up before you notarize.

Colorado courts have occasionally accepted a street address plus a sufficiently specific identifying description in lieu of the formal legal description, but the legal description is the safer choice. Every county assessor's website provides it for free; the Appendix B section of this packet explains how to find yours.

## Notarization

The lien statement must be sworn before a notary public. You do **not** sign before you see the notary — the notary witnesses your signature. Bring:
- Your unsigned lien statement.
- A government-issued photo ID (driver's license, passport).
- Payment (usually $5–$25).

The notary will ask you to swear or affirm that the contents are true, watch you sign, and apply the notary seal and their signature. See the Notary Guide section for details.

## Filing fee

The filing fee varies by Colorado county. Most counties charge a base fee for the first page plus a per-page fee for additional pages. Typical totals for a 1–2 page lien statement run $13–$30. Confirm the exact fee by calling the recorder's office before you go, or by checking Appendix A — County Filing Directory.

## How to file

Most Colorado counties accept filings in one of three ways:
1. **In person** at the county recorder's office during business hours (usually Monday–Friday, generally 8:00–4:00 or 8:00–5:00, with some counties closing earlier on Fridays — hours vary by county, so call the specific recorder before you go (see Appendix A)).
2. **By mail**, with a check for the filing fee and a self-addressed stamped envelope for the recorded copy to be returned.
3. **Electronically** through a state or county e-recording system. Not every county offers this. Per-county e-recording availability changes frequently; confirm directly with the county recorder or the e-recording service (Simplifile, CSC, eRecording Partners Network) before you rely on it.

## After filing

- Keep your recorded copy (you'll get a stamped page back with the reception number and recording date).
- Send a copy of the recorded lien to the property owner by certified mail with return receipt requested. This is not strictly required by statute, but it is nearly universal practice and sometimes prompts the owner to pay to clear the title.
- Calendar the 6-month foreclosure deadline. If you are not prepared to sue to foreclose, get an attorney engaged now — foreclosure is generally a district court lawsuit, though county court may have jurisdiction when the amount at issue is within its statutory cap.

## Customer verification callouts

Your lien statement document contains yellow "CUSTOMER VERIFICATION REQUIRED" callouts for:
- The statutory opening language (per § 38-22-109(1)).
- The sworn verification / jurat block format.
- Any missing legal description or owner name block from your intake.

Verify each one against the current statute before notarizing and filing. If you are unsure, stop and call a Colorado-licensed attorney.
