# Appendix B — Legal Description Lookup

A Colorado lien statement requires the **legal description** of the property per C.R.S. § 38-22-109(1) — read the current statute text at **https://leg.colorado.gov/colorado-revised-statutes**.

The legal description — not the street address alone. This section explains what a legal description is, where to find it, and what to do if you cannot.

## What a legal description is

A legal description is the formal, unique identifier for a parcel of land. It takes one of two forms in Colorado:

1. **Lot / Block / Subdivision** — the most common form in cities and newer residential developments. Example format: "Lot 4, Block 3, Downtown Subdivision, filed August 1, 1952, Reception No. 12345, City and County of Denver."

2. **Metes and bounds** — the older form, common for rural parcels and irregular lots. Reads as a series of compass directions and distances: "Commencing at the Northwest corner of Section 5, Township 2 South, Range 68 West, thence East 330 feet, thence South 160 feet..." These are long and technical. If your property has a metes-and-bounds description, copy it exactly from the source document.

The street address (e.g. "900 Market Street") is not a legal description. You need the lot/block/subdivision or the metes-and-bounds text.

## Where to find the legal description

Ranked by ease:

- **Property tax statement or county assessor website** — free, easiest. Every Colorado county assessor publishes parcel records online, searchable by street address. The legal description is on the parcel detail page. Appendix A in this packet lists the URLs.
- **Your deed (if you are the owner)** — recorded in the deed that transferred the property to you. Deed lookups are free at the county recorder's website.
- **Title insurance policy** — Schedule A of a title policy contains the legal description. If you were the general contractor on new construction, your project paperwork may include a title commitment with this information.
- **The owner's lender** — the mortgage on the property includes the legal description. Useful only if you have a contact at the lender; most lenders will not volunteer this without a subpoena.

## Step-by-step: Denver County example

1. Go to **https://www.denvergov.org/property/realproperty** (also reachable via denvergov.org → Property).
2. Enter the street address.
3. Open the parcel detail page.
4. Find the "Legal Description" field. Copy it verbatim — including punctuation, numbers, and abbreviations.
5. Paste into the legal description field of your lien document, replacing any CUSTOMER VERIFICATION callout that appeared there.

The other 14 supported Colorado counties follow the same pattern. See Appendix A — County Filing Directory for each county's assessor URL.

## What to do if you cannot find it

Some possibilities:

- **Call the county assessor's office.** Most will give the legal description over the phone once you give them a street address. Calls are typically handled in under 5 minutes. No charge.
- **Request a copy of the deed** from the county recorder. Fees vary but typically under $20.
- **Order a title search** from a local title company. $100–$300. Overkill for most kits; reasonable if the property has a complex title history.

## What happens if you file without a legal description

Colorado courts have occasionally accepted a lien statement that identifies the property by street address plus a sufficient identifying description (e.g. "the improved real property known as 900 Market Street, Denver, Colorado, being the property on which the claimant performed the labor described herein, consisting of a three-story office building"). This is weaker than a formal legal description. Most contractors in this situation take the extra hour to find the legal description rather than rely on the street-address fallback.

If you decide to file without the legal description, expect the lien to be more vulnerable to attack. Most contractors in this situation consult a Colorado-licensed attorney before taking the street-address-only route.

If you have questions about this step, call a Colorado-licensed attorney.
